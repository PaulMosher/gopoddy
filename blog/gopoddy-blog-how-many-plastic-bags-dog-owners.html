How Many Plastic Bags Do Dog Owners Use Per Year?

It starts with one walk. One dog. One bag. Tied, tossed, forgotten.

Multiply that by 90 million dogs. Multiply that by 365 days. Multiply that by the average number of times a dog goes per walk. And you arrive at a number that most people — even environmentally conscious dog owners — have never stopped to calculate.

27,000,000,000 Plastic bags used by U.S. dog owners every year for pet waste

Twenty-three billion. That's the estimated number of single-use plastic bags used annually in the United States for dog waste disposal. Every single one of them gets used once, for approximately 60 seconds, before being tied shut and sent to a landfill where it will persist — largely intact — for hundreds of years.

Let's put that number in context.

What 23 Billion Bags Actually Looks Like

Numbers at this scale are hard to visualize. Here are a few comparisons that make the magnitude concrete:

  • If you laid 27 billion plastic bags end to end, they would circle the Earth more than 105,000 times.
  • 27 billion bags weigh approximately 270,000 tons — roughly the weight of 1,800 fully loaded Boeing 747s.
  • At a rate of one bag per second, it would take more than 728 years to count them all.
  • The non-degradable bag segment alone — 78.8% of the market — represents over 18 billion bags per year made entirely from fossil fuel-derived plastic.
  • The global plastic bag market includes shopping bags, produce bags, and dozens of other categories — and dog waste bags are a meaningful fraction of total single-use plastic bag consumption.

For context: The global dog poop bags market was valued at $152.7 million in 2022 and is projected to reach $208.2 million by 2032 — growing at 3.1% annually. The market exists entirely to serve a habit that no longer needs to exist.

Where Does All That Plastic Go?

The short answer: landfills. And once there, it stays.

Only 9% of all plastic ever produced has been recycled. The rest — roughly 91% — has gone to landfills, incineration, or the natural environment. Thin-film plastics like dog waste bags are among the hardest to recycle: most municipal recycling programs don't accept them, they jam sorting equipment, and the economics of recycling them are unfavorable.

A standard HDPE plastic bag in a landfill will take between 10 and 1,000 years to fully degrade — estimates vary based on conditions, but "hundreds of years" is the accepted range. During that time, the bag doesn't disappear — it fragments into smaller and smaller pieces of microplastic, which persist in soil and water essentially indefinitely.

The biological waste inside the bag fares no better. Landfills are engineered to minimize decomposition — they're sealed, compacted environments with limited oxygen and moisture. Dog waste entombed in a plastic bag in a landfill doesn't get treated. It simply sits there, alongside the bag, for generations.

Now scale that. If just 1% of U.S. dog owners switched to a bag-free system, that's approximately 900,000 households — eliminating roughly 657 million bags per year.

If 10% switched: 6.6 billion bags eliminated annually.

The math of individual action at scale is actually quite powerful. The plastic bag habit is not inevitable — it's just what everyone does because a better alternative hasn't been widely available. That's changing.

Why Biodegradable Bags Don't Solve the Scale Problem

One response to the 27 billion bag figure is to switch to biodegradable or compostable alternatives. The intention is right. The outcome, in most real-world conditions, is not.

Biodegradable bags require oxygen, sunlight, moisture, and microbial activity to break down — conditions that don't exist in a landfill. Compostable bags require industrial composting facilities operating at 130–160°F — facilities that most municipalities don't have, and that don't accept pet waste in most places that do.

Swapping 27 billion standard plastic bags for 27 billion biodegradable bags doesn't solve the scale problem. It slightly changes the material while leaving the underlying system — use once, discard, landfill — completely intact.

The most effective response to a 27 billion bag problem is not a better bag. It's no bag.

The Opportunity: What Elimination Looks Like

The good news embedded in the 27 billion figure is that it represents an enormous, largely untouched opportunity for reduction. Unlike many environmental problems — where the solution requires systemic infrastructure changes or major behavioral shifts — this one is solvable at the individual level, one walk at a time.

A reusable collection and disposal system replaces every bag a dog owner would otherwise use. Not 50% of them. Not the ones on short walks or easy days. All of them — in all weather, in all seasons, on every walk regardless of what happens.

One device. One simple routine. Zero bags.

Over a dog's lifetime, that's 7,300 bags that never get made, never get used, and never go to a landfill. Multiplied across even a fraction of the 90 million dogs in the United States, the numbers become genuinely significant.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many plastic bags do dog owners use per year in the U.S.?

Approximately 27 billion single-use plastic bags are used annually by U.S. dog owners for pet waste disposal. This figure is derived from estimates based on the U.S. dog population (approximately 90 million dogs — per APPA 2026), average waste frequency, and market data from the dog poop bag industry.

How many bags does one dog go through in a year?

The average dog owner uses approximately 365–730 bags per year, depending on how frequently their dog defecates per walk and how many walks they take daily. A conservative estimate of one bag per day yields 365 bags per year. Many dogs require more.

Are dog poop bags recyclable?

Almost never in practice. Most curbside recycling programs don't accept thin-film plastics like dog waste bags. Even those with recycling symbols typically cannot be processed by standard municipal recycling equipment. Some grocery stores offer plastic bag drop-off programs, but these programs explicitly exclude dog waste bags for hygiene reasons.

How long does a plastic dog poop bag take to decompose?

Standard plastic bags take between 10 and 1,000 years to fully degrade in a landfill, depending on conditions. During this time they fragment into microplastics rather than breaking down into harmless components. "Biodegradable" bags take longer than their marketing implies when landfilled, because landfills lack the conditions needed for meaningful biodegradation.

What would happen if all dog owners stopped using plastic bags?

If every U.S. dog owner eliminated single-use plastic bags from their routine — using a reusable system and flushing waste where permitted — approximately 27 billion plastic bags per year would be eliminated from landfills. The biological waste itself would be routed through wastewater treatment infrastructure, reducing the pathogen load in waterways and groundwater. The environmental benefit would be immediate and cumulative.

Is there a dog waste bag alternative that uses no plastic at all?

Yes. A reusable, sealed retrieval device collects waste during the walk without a bag of any kind. The waste is transported home and emptied into the toilet, where it's processed by municipal wastewater treatment — the same infrastructure used for human waste. No bag. No landfill. No recurring cost.
