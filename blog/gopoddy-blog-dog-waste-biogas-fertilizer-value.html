Dog Poop as a Resource: How Rerouting Pet Waste Could Generate Over $1 Billion in Energy and Fertilizer

By Go Poddy • April 2026 • Updated with addressable market correction

Dog waste is one of the most overlooked environmental resources in the United States. Properly rerouted through existing wastewater treatment infrastructure, it could generate over $1.19 billion in annual value from renewable energy and agricultural fertilizer. Nobody in the pet industry is talking about this. We are.

Most conversations about dog waste focus on what it costs: the plastic bags, the municipal cleanup programs, the water contamination. Those are real problems worth solving. But there is another side to this story that almost nobody has examined — what dog waste could be worth if we stopped sending it to landfills and started routing it where it belongs.

This analysis is Go Poddy's original work, built from publicly available data from the Argonne National Laboratory, the American Biogas Council, the U.S. Energy Information Administration, and Oklahoma State University Extension. We welcome scrutiny. Transparent math is the only kind we use.

First: A Note on the Numbers

The United States has approximately 90 million dogs (APPA 2026; AVMA 2025: 87.3 million). But not all of those dogs are relevant to this analysis.

Roughly 20% of U.S. dog owners live in rural areas — on farms, acreages, and properties where dogs roam freely and waste dispersal happens naturally across open land (YouGov 2023). Realistically, these owners are unlikely to adopt a waste-collection system, and that is fine. The environmental calculus is genuinely different on ten acres than it is on a quarter-acre suburban lot or a city sidewalk.

That leaves approximately 72 million dogs in suburban and urban environments — dogs whose owners walk them daily in shared public spaces where waste management matters. This is the addressable market for Go Poddy, and it is the basis for every figure in this analysis. We believe in honest math.

Where Dog Waste Goes Today

Of the 9.9 million tons of waste produced annually by suburban and urban dogs, virtually none reaches a wastewater treatment plant. Instead it goes one of two places: into a plastic bag and then a landfill, or directly onto the ground where it washes into local waterways as a nonpoint source pollutant.

The EPA classifies dog waste as an environmental hazard on par with pesticides and toxic chemicals. Two to three days of waste from just 100 dogs can temporarily close a bay within 25 miles to swimming and fishing. The contamination is real, the infrastructure to handle it already exists, and almost nobody is using it.

Municipal wastewater treatment systems are designed to process biological waste. The EPA explicitly recommends flushing dog waste as a safe disposal method. Dog waste contains similar pathogens to human waste — the same infrastructure handles both. The only thing missing is the delivery mechanism. That is what the Poddy provides.

The Resource Recovery Case

When biological waste enters a wastewater treatment plant, it does not simply disappear. It is processed into two valuable outputs: biogas (a form of renewable energy) and biosolids (an agricultural fertilizer). Both have measurable market value. Both are currently being wasted because dog waste never reaches the plant.

What wastewater treatment plants actually do

Approximately 10% of U.S. wastewater treatment plants use anaerobic digestion to capture biogas from the organic material they process, according to Argonne National Laboratory and ScienceDirect. The American Biogas Council reports approximately 860 plants are actively capturing and using the biogas they produce.

Separately, 70% of the biosolids produced at wastewater treatment plants — the solid material remaining after treatment — are land-applied as agricultural fertilizer, according to Oklahoma State University Extension and EPA data. This represents full resource recovery from the waste stream, regardless of whether a given plant also captures biogas.

Why This Has Never Been Done Before

Because there was no practical way to get it there. Picking up waste in a plastic bag and carrying it home to flush it is unpleasant, inconvenient, and creates exactly the plastic waste problem it is trying to solve. The bag itself was always the barrier.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it legal to flush dog waste?

The EPA recommends flushing as a safe disposal method. Municipal wastewater systems are designed for biological waste and process dog waste the same way they process human waste. Some municipalities restrict it — always check local regulations. Go Poddy plans to maintain a searchable database of local guidelines at gopoddy.com.

Does all the resource value actually get captured?

No — and we are not claiming it does. Our $1.19 billion figure is a conservative estimate of the potential value if infrastructure were fully utilized. Today, only 10% of wastewater treatment plants actively capture biogas. But all plants produce biosolids, and 70% of those are land-applied as fertilizer. The value is real. The capture rate will grow as infrastructure investment increases.

Why exclude rural dogs?

Rural dog owners — approximately 18 million dogs — typically have land where waste can disperse naturally, and most do not walk their dogs on leashes in shared public spaces. Requiring or expecting pickup behavior from this segment is unrealistic. Our analysis reflects who is actually addressable, not a theoretical maximum designed to make the numbers look bigger.

How does Go Poddy connect to all of this?

The Poddy is the delivery mechanism. It is the only product designed to bring dog waste from the sidewalk to the wastewater treatment plant in a clean, reusable, odor-proof container. Without it, the infrastructure exists but has no practical pathway to receive the material. With it, every suburban and urban dog walk becomes an opportunity to close the loop.
